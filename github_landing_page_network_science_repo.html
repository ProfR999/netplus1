Introduction to Networks & Graph Theory

Course Module • Prepared by Prof. Reyes • CC BY-NC 4.0 Licensed

Session Abstract

Networks represent the structural skeleton of complex systems. From social networks and information cascades to power grids and metabolic pathing, understanding the foundational principles of graph theory allows us to systematically analyze how interactions shape macroscopic behaviors.

1. What is a Network?

Mathematically, a network is represented as a graph $G = (V, E)$, which is a structure consisting of a set of elements called vertices (or nodes) $V$, joined by a set of connections called edges (or links) $E$.

G = (V, E)
Where $V = \{v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n\}$ and $E \subseteq \{(v_i, v_j) \mid v_i, v_j \in V\}$

2. Structural Classification of Graphs

Depending on the properties of the relationships (edges) between elements, networks are classified into several configurations:

Directed vs. Undirected

In undirected graphs, links are symmetric (e.g., peer friendships). In directed graphs (digraphs), relationships flow one-way, meaning $(v_i, v_j) \neq (v_j, v_i)$ (e.g., following on social media).

Weighted vs. Unweighted

In weighted graphs, each link carries a numerical value representing distance, capacity, intensity, or cost. In unweighted networks, edges denote binary existence ($0$ or $1$).

Bipartite Networks

A network where nodes are partitioned into two disjoint sets, say $U$ and $V$, such that every edge connects a node in $U$ to one in $V$. No links exist between nodes of the same set (e.g., authors and publications).

Sparse vs. Dense

A graph is dense if the actual number of edges $|E|$ approaches the maximum possible edges, i.e., $|E| \approx \frac{|V|(|V|-1)}{2}$. Real-world large networks are almost universally sparse.

3. Key Topology & Network Metrics

To evaluate the role of single elements or characterize macroscopic topological features, we utilize key mathematical equations:

Degree and Degree Distribution ($k_i$)

The degree $k_i$ of a node $i$ is the count of edges connected to it. In a directed network, we measure In-degree ($k_i^{in}$) and Out-degree ($k_i^{out}$).

$$k_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N} A_{ij}$$

Average Path Length ($L$)

Calculates the mean shortest path distance between all node pairs. It shows the speed of navigation or flow over the network structure.

$$L = \frac{1}{N(N-1)} \sum_{i \neq j} d(i, j)$$

Clustering Coefficient ($C_i$)

Reflects the transitivity of linkages, measuring the ratio of closed triangles centered at node $i$ relative to the total possible connections amongst its immediate neighbors.

$$C_i = \frac{2 T_i}{k_i(k_i - 1)}$$
